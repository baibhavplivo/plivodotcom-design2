It starts when the customer first reaches out for help and ends when their issue is fully resolved.</p><p>This number shows how smoothly your support team works.</p><p>If the time to resolution is low, your team is solving problems fast and keeping customers happy.&nbsp;</p><p>And if it’s high, it means something’s going wrong; maybe your team is stuck with slow tools, confusing steps, or too many back-and-forth messages.</p><table>

</p>
   </td>
   </tr></tbody></table><p>The key is to use the metric of time to resolution to identify and resolve issues, not just to track them.</p><h2>Why is time to resolution a key customer service metric?</h2><p>Time to resolution is a key customer service metric that impacts efficiency, customer experience, and operations. Here's why it matters:</p><h3>1. Saves time</h3><p>No one likes to wait. Not your customers, not your team. Time to resolution matters because it’s the ultimate middle ground for valuing your customers’ time and your team’s effort.</p><p>Optimizing time to resolution means you’re building a business that runs smoother, retains more customers, and spends smarter.</p><h3>2. Increases satisfaction</h3><p>Time to resolution is the invisible thread connecting how fast you fix problems to how happy your customers feel.</p><p>When you close tickets quickly, you’re not just checking a box, you’re telling customers, “Your time matters to us.” This builds trust.</p><p>But speed isn’t the whole story. First response time (FRT), which refers to how quickly you acknowledge a customer’s request, sets the tone for the entire interaction.&nbsp;</p><p>Imagine waiting hours just to hear, “We’re on it!” Even if you fix the problem later, that initial delay leaves customers feeling ignored. That’s why first call resolution (FCR) matters.&nbsp;</p><p>Solving an issue on the first try without transfers or follow-ups:</p><ul><li>Saves customers from repeating themselves.</li><li>Frees up your team to handle new issues.</li></ul><p>Boosts satisfaction as every <a href="https://www.sqmgroup.com/resources/library/blog/FCR-benchmarking-industry-results-2021">1% improvement in FCR</a> drives customer satisfaction score (CSAT) by 1-5%.</p><table>
<tbody><tr><td>Companies that master FCR are <a href="https://www.forrester.com/blogs/whats-the-roi-of-cx-transformation/">2.4x more likely</a> to keep customers and more likely to get good reviews. 
<p></p>
   </td>
</tr></tbody></table><p>Why? Because fixing things fast and right the first time shows you’re reliable, which fosters loyalty.</p><h3>3. Tracks performance</h3><p>While other metrics might focus on quick replies like “We’re looking into it!”, time to resolution shows whether the solution actually worked. This makes teams responsible for solving problems completely, not just responding to them.</p><p>Customers care most about results, not promises. A fast reply might seem helpful, but if the fix is rushed or incomplete, trust disappears quickly.&nbsp;</p><table>

   <td>Time to resolution prevents teams from cutting corners by tracking the time it takes to deliver a final, working solution.

Spots problems</h3><p>Tracking time to resolution is a clear way to find out why your team struggles. Better training and clearer instructions.</p><p>Now, let’s say your team has too many tickets to handle or new hires take 3-6 months to ramp up. Meanwhile, teams with higher time to resolution might need extra training or tools to catch up.</p><p>These comparisons also expose hidden patterns.</p><p>Let’s say a specific product always has slower fixes. This could mean that the design confuses customers or the instructions are unclear. Updating product guides or simplifying its features could cut future support tickets and boost satisfaction.</p><h2>How to calculate time to resolution</h2><p>The formula for calculating time to resolution is:</p><table><tbody><tr><td><strong>Average TTR =</strong> Total time spent resolving all issues / Number of issues solved
   </td>
   </tr></tbody></table><p>Let’s break this down:</p><p><strong>Total time spent resolving all issues: </strong>Add up all the minutes, hours, or days your team spent fixing customer problems in a specific period (like a day, week, or month).</p><p>For example, if a ticket took two hours to resolve, and another took four hours, your total time is six hours.</p><p><strong>Number of issues solved:</strong> Count how many customer problems your team fully resolved in that same period.</p><p>If you resolve three tickets in a day, your number of solved issues is three.</p><p>To put it all together, divide the total time by the number of solved issues to obtain your average TTR.</p>So, for example, let’s say your software company’s support team resolved five tickets yesterday:
<p></p>
<ul>

<li><strong>Ticket 1: </strong>One hour</li>

<li><strong>Ticket 2:</strong> Three hours</li>

<li><strong>Ticket 3: </strong>Two hours</li>

<li><strong>Ticket 4: </strong>Six hours</li>

<li><strong>Ticket 5:</strong> Two hours

<p>
Total time spent = 1 + 3 + 2 + 6 + 2 = 14 hours
</p>
<p>
Number of resolved tickets = 5
</p>
<p>
Average TTR = 14 hours / 5 tickets = 2.8 hours per ticket
</p></li>
</ul>
   <p>This means, on average, your team takes 2.8 hours to fully resolve a customer issue.&nbsp;</p><p>If this number feels too high, it’s a sign to dig deeper, maybe some tickets are getting stuck in endless back-and-forth, or your team needs better tools to speed things up.</p><h2>6 Ways to leverage AI to reduce time to resolution</h2><p>Support delays rarely stem from a lack of effort — they often result from noise: an excessive number of tickets, insufficient context, and tasks that should never be manual.</p><p>Artificial intelligence changes that by streamlining what gets in the way.</p><p>Here’s how to use AI to speed up support and cut resolution times:</p><h3>1.
