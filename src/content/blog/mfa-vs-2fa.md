<p>Passwords have been the default security tool for years. But here’s the problem: they’re not enough anymore.</p><p>In 2021, <a href="https://www.lastpass.com/-/media/9FE0BF5DC473413B8AB4DF3BD8688295.pdf">85% of data breaches</a> happened because of phishing or stolen passwords.</p><p>The lesson? If you’re <em>only</em> using passwords, you’re leaving the door wide open for attackers.</p><p>That’s why two-factor authentication (2FA) and multi-factor authentication (MFA) exist. These tools add extra layers of security. In fact, studies show 2FA stops <a href="https://cloud.google.com/blog/products/identity-security/protect-users-in-your-apps-with-multi-factor-authentication">96% of bulk phishing attacks</a>, whereas MFA is capable of blocking <a href="https://query.prod.cms.rt.microsoft.com/cms/api/am/binary/RW166lD#:~:text=Accounts%20with%20Known%20Leaked%20Credentials,designed%20for%20multi%2D%20factor%20authentication.">99.9% of automated account breaches</a>.</p><p>But not every business needs the same level of protection. A small online store won’t need the same setup as a hospital handling patient records.</p><p>This article breaks down how 2FA and MFA work, where they fit in 2025, and how to choose the right approach for your needs.</p><h2>What are 2FA and MFA?</h2><p>You’ve probably come across <a href="https://www.plivo.com/use-case/two-factor-authentication/">2FA</a> and MFA when setting up security for your accounts. They both add extra protection, but what exactly do they mean? Let’s break them down and see how they work.</p><h3>2FA: Two steps, stronger security</h3><p>2FA does exactly what the name says: it adds <em>two</em> steps to verify your identity. Here’s how it works:</p><ol><li><strong>Step 1: </strong>You enter your password (something you <em>know</em>).</li><li><strong>Step 2: </strong>You provide a second proof, like a one-time code sent via SMS or email (something you <em>have</em>).</li></ol><p>For example, when you log into your bank account, you might type your password and then get a text message with a 6-digit code. Without that code, hackers can’t access your account — even if they steal your password.</p><p>2FA isn’t bulletproof, but it’s a massive upgrade over passwords alone. It’s like adding a deadbolt to a door: not impossible to break, but way harder for intruders.</p><h3>MFA: More layers, more security</h3><p>MFA takes the idea further. Instead of just <em>two</em> steps, it uses <em>two </em>or <em>more</em> <strong>different</strong> types of verification. These fall into three categories:</p><ul><li><strong>Something you know</strong> (password, PIN).</li><li><strong>Something you have</strong> (phone, hardware token).</li><li><strong>Something you are</strong> (fingerprint, face scan).</li></ul><p>For instance, logging into a corporate system might require:</p><ol><li>A password (<em>something you know</em>),</li><li>A fingerprint scan (<em>something you are</em>),</li><li>And a code from a physical security key (<em>something you have</em>).</li></ol><p>MFA’s flexibility makes it ideal for high-risk scenarios. Banks, healthcare systems, and government agencies use it because it’s tough for attackers to bypass multiple layers.</p><h2>2FA vs. MFA: Which one do you need?</h2><p>Understanding when 2FA and MFA work best and where they might fall short sets the stage for understanding which method fits into your security plan.</p><p>Let's see how these authentication methods shine in various scenarios.</p><h3>When 2FA shines</h3><p>2FA isn’t a one-size-fits-all solution, but in the right situations, it’s a game-changer. Let’s break down where it adds the most value:</p><h4>Low-risk applications</h4><p>For platforms that don’t handle highly sensitive data (think streaming services, newsletters, or forums), 2FA strikes a perfect balance.</p><p>It adds an important layer of security without overwhelming users. For example, a fitness app storing workout logs might use 2FA to protect user accounts but skip more complex MFA setups.</p><h4>Customer-facing workflows</h4><p>E-commerce sites, travel booking platforms, and subscription services benefit from 2FA because it secures transactions without disrupting the user experience.</p><p>Imagine a customer checking out on an online store: a quick SMS code or email OTP keeps their payment details safe without adding friction.</p><h3>Challenges with 2FA</h3><p>While 2FA is effective, it’s not flawless. Here are the hurdles businesses often face:</p><h4>Implementation costs</h4><p>Smaller businesses might struggle with the upfront costs of SMS gateways, authenticator apps, or API integrations. Maintenance also adds up — monitoring delivery rates, handling failed OTPs, and updating systems as threats evolve.</p><h4>User resistance</h4><p>Even a few extra seconds during login can frustrate users.</p><p>For example, if a verification code takes too long to arrive, customers might abandon a signup flow. Worse, some users may disable 2FA altogether if the process feels overly difficult.</p><h4>Device dependency risks</h4><p>If a user loses their phone or can’t access their email, account recovery becomes a headache.&nbsp;</p><p>Support teams often field urgent requests like, “I changed my number, how do I log in now?” Without backup options (like backup codes or alternate verification methods), businesses risk locking out legitimate users.</p><h4>Security gaps in SMS-based 2FA</h4><p>SMS codes, while convenient, are vulnerable to <a href="https://www.plivo.com/blog/sim-swapping-fraud/">SIM-swapping attacks</a> or phishing. If a hacker hijacks a user’s phone number, they can intercept OTPs and bypass 2FA.</p><p>This is why industries like banking are moving toward app-based authenticators or hardware keys.</p><h4>Accessibility issues</h4><p>Not all users have smartphones or reliable internet. Relying solely on SMS or authenticator apps can exclude people in areas with poor connectivity or those using older devices.</p><h3>Best practices for effective 2FA</h3><p>To overcome these challenges, businesses need a strategic approach. Here’s how to make 2FA work <em>for</em> you, not against you:</p><h4>Prioritize user education</h4><p>Explain why 2FA matters. A short tutorial during signup (“This keeps your account safe from hackers”) or a friendly email reminder (“Your OTP is on the way!”) can reduce resistance.&nbsp;</p><p>Transparency builds trust — users are more likely to comply if they understand the benefits.</p><h4>Simplify the login experience</h4><p>Simplifying the login process starts with small changes that make a big difference.</p><p>For example, let users sign in with their email address instead of forcing them to remember a complicated username. Since everyone already knows their email, this cuts down on forgotten login details.</p><p>Next, avoid overwhelming users with strict password rules. Yes, strong passwords matter, but forcing a reset every 30 days? That’s a recipe for frustration.</p><p>Pair 2FA with simple password guidelines (like a minimum length) to keep accounts secure without annoying users.</p><h4>Offer multiple verification options</h4><p>Not everyone wants SMS. Provide alternatives like:</p><ul><li><strong>Email OTPs</strong> (for users without smartphones).</li><li><strong>Authenticator apps</strong> (like Google Authenticator for tech-savvy users).</li><li><strong>Backup codes</strong> (for emergencies when devices are lost).</li></ul><h4>Plan for account recovery</h4><p>Don’t wait for lockout panics. Offer:</p><ul><li><strong>Backup codes:</strong> Let users download or print them during setup.</li><li><strong>Alternate contact methods: </strong>Allow users to add a backup email or security questions.</li><li><strong>24/7 support: </strong>Ensure users can quickly reach help if they’re stuck.</li></ul><h4>Balance security and convenience</h4><p>For low-risk apps, allow users to “trust this device” for 30 days. This way, they only need to complete 2FA once per month on their personal devices.</p><h3>How Plivo simplifies secure 2FA</h3><p>Plivo helps businesses stop hackers in their tracks without adding friction for users. MFA isn’t always necessary, but in certain situations, it can be a smart safeguard. Here’s when it makes sense to use it.</p><h4>Regulated industries</h4><p>Financial institutions, healthcare providers, and government agencies often need to comply with strict requirements, mandating advanced security measures.</p><h4>Critical systems</h4><p>MFA is essential when securing servers, databases, admin panels, or tools that store intellectual property, customer data, or financial records. A breach in these systems can lead to significant financial and reputational harm.</p><h3>Challenges with MFA</h3><p><a href="https://support.plivo.com/hc/en-us/articles/360041766411-How-can-I-enable-two-factor-authentication">Implementing MFA</a> comes with hurdles that require proactive management:</p><h4>Device limitations</h4><p>Older smartphones, legacy systems, or devices without biometric sensors (e.g., fingerprint scanners) may not support modern MFA methods like app-based authenticators or hardware tokens. Employees using unsupported devices risk being locked out.</p><h4>Complex setup processes</h4><p>Users often struggle with configuring MFA tools, especially without clear, role-specific guides. For example, setting up a YubiKey on a macOS device requires different steps than on Windows, and unclear instructions may lead to errors or abandonment.</p><h4>Low user adoption</h4><p>Without training, employees may view MFA as an inconvenience. Remote workers, in particular, might avoid using unfamiliar tools like biometrics or security keys if they lack guidance.</p><h4>Security workarounds</h4><p>Frustrated users may share passwords, reuse weak credentials, or disable MFA entirely to speed up logins — defeating the purpose of enhanced security.</p><h4>Inconsistent user experience</h4><p>Poorly integrated <a href="https://www.plivo.com/blog/best-multi-factor-authentication-solutions-for-business/">MFA solutions</a> can disrupt workflows.</p><p>For example, requiring a hardware token, password, and SMS code for routine tasks slows productivity and fuels resentment.</p><h3>Best practices for effective MFA</h3><p>To tackle these challenges, businesses need a clear strategy. Here’s how to make MFA work in your favor:</p><h4>Provide multiple authentication options</h4><p>It’s important to offer a variety of authentication methods because not everyone uses the same devices or has the same tech skills.</p><p>Support diverse user needs and devices by offering:</p><ul><li><strong>SMS/email OTPs: </strong>For employees with basic mobile phones or limited tech access.</li><li><strong>Authenticator apps: </strong>For users comfortable with smartphones.</li><li><strong>Biometrics:</strong> Fingerprint or facial recognition for modern devices.</li><li><strong>Hardware tokens: </strong>Physical keys for high-risk roles like system admins.</li></ul><h4>Implement role-based training programs</h4><p>Training isn’t one-size-fits-all. Different teams have different needs, so your training should reflect that.</p><p>IT teams, for example, need detailed guides for setting up hardware tokens on different operating systems, like “How to Configure YubiKey on Windows 11.” Field employees, who might not have time for lengthy tutorials, benefit from short video demos showing how to approve logins via SMS or authenticator apps.</p><p>Executives, who often switch devices or travel, need personalized 1:1 sessions to make MFA setup hassle-free.</p><h4>Enforce organization-wide MFA policies</h4><p>If MFA is optional, attackers will target the weakest link. That’s why it should be enforced for everyone — employees, contractors, freelancers, and even legacy systems. Granting temporary access to third parties? They should use MFA too.</p><p>Outdated tools that don’t support MFA? Update or replace them. Regular audits help ensure no one is bypassing the rules.</p><h4>Deploy adaptive MFA</h4><p>Not every login needs the same level of security. Adaptive MFA adjusts based on the risk level of the situation.</p><p>For routine tasks, like checking email from a trusted device, a password and SMS code are enough. But for high-risk scenarios like logging in from a new country or accessing financial systems, additional checks, such as biometrics or hardware tokens, kick in.</p><h4>Integrate MFA with single sign-on</h4><p>Integrating MFA with single sign-on (SSO) enhances security without adding extra steps for users.</p><p>With SSO, they authenticate once using MFA and gain access to multiple systems without needing to log in repeatedly. This not only strengthens protection but also improves the user experience by reducing login fatigue.</p><h2>Trends shaping authentication in 2025</h2><p>As we move further into the digital world, the way we verify our identities is changing quickly. By 2025, new trends will change how people and companies confirm who they are and keep their information secure.</p><p>Here’s a look at the most important trends in authentication for 2025:</p><h3>Passwordless authentication is on rise</h3><p>Passwordless methods, like scanning your fingerprint, are faster and easier. Imagine logging into your account with a quick face scan instead of typing out a long password.</p><p>It’s not just convenient — it’s what people want. In fact, <a href="https://www.pymnts.com/wp-content/uploads/2023/01/PYMNTS-Consumer-Authentication-Preferences-January-2023.pdf">52% of consumers</a> prefer biometrics over traditional authentication methods. It’s no surprise this trend is picking up speed.</p><p>Businesses have also realized they need something better.</p><p>Already, <a href="https://www.okta.com/sites/default/files/2021-03/IDG-Passwordless-The-Future-of-User-Authentication.pdf">33% of IT departments globally</a> are using passwordless authentication, and over <a href="https://www.statista.com/statistics/1441237/future-adoption-of-passwordless-authentication-by-various-industries-worldwide/">one-third of companies</a> are planning to adopt it in the near future. That’s a clear sign that companies see this as the future of security.</p><p>Plus, enterprises are already leading the way. For example, Microsoft reported <a href="https://www.astuteanalytica.com/industry-report/passwordless-authentication-market">2 million monthly passwordless sign-ins</a> through Windows Hello in early 2023. When tech giants like Microsoft push passwordless options, it’s a signal to everyone else: this is the direction we’re heading.</p><h3>AI threats demand smarter defenses</h3><p>AI-generated deepfakes are already posing serious security risks, and the problem is only growing.</p><p>By 2026, experts predict that <a href="https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2024-02-01-gartner-predicts-30-percent-of-enterprises-will-consider-identity-verification-and-authentication-solutions-unreliable-in-isolation-due-to-deepfakes-by-2026">30% of businesses</a> will no longer trust traditional identity verification methods due to the rise of AI-driven fraud. This is especially concerning for systems that rely on facial recognition or voice authentication, as deepfakes allow attackers to impersonate individuals and bypass security measures.</p><p>Financial institutions are particularly vulnerable — fraudulent identities can erode trust and compromise sensitive transactions. To counter this, businesses must adopt AI-powered tools capable of detecting deepfakes in real-time before they cause damage.</p><p>Beyond security, deepfakes also raise legal and ethical concerns.</p><p>As AI-generated content becomes more sophisticated, the reliability of biometric data declines, prompting discussions about data collection, storage, and privacy. Regulations similar to the General Data Protection Regulation (EU) (GDPR) may emerge to address these risks, ensuring stricter safeguards for personal information and reducing the potential for misuse.</p><h3>Behavioural biometrics</h3><p>What if your identity wasn’t based on a password you could forget or a fingerprint that could be faked, but on something uniquely yours — your online behavior?</p><p>That’s what behavioral biometrics is all about. It tracks how fast you type, the way you move your mouse, or even how you swipe on your phone to figure out if it’s really you.</p><p>This trend is picking up steam because it’s tough for hackers to mimic these unique habits, making it a powerful way to keep your digital life secure. <em>Why is this so important?</em></p><p>Well, businesses and banks are jumping on board to protect millions of users. Take the Royal Bank of Scotland, for example. They’re using it to safeguard <a href="https://www.intechopen.com/chapters/80748">18.7 million accounts</a> by analyzing how people type and swipe.</p><p>It’s not just banks either; schools are using it to stop cheating in online exams by watching how students interact with their devices. The numbers back this up too — the market for this tech is projected to climb to <a href="https://www.grandviewresearch.com/press-release/global-behavioral-biometrics-market">$9.92 billion </a>by 2030.</p><h3>Decentralized identity</h3><p>Picture this: instead of tech giants or governments holding all your personal info, you keep it in a secure digital wallet on your phone. That's a decentralized identity in a nutshell. So, why is this trend blowing up, and why should you care?</p><p>The rise is driven by a few key shifts. For one, cyberattacks are out of control. This makes those old-school, centralized databases look like easy targets.</p><p>People are also fed up with having no say over their data; they want to decide who sees what. Plus, new tech like blockchain is making this whole idea work better than ever. It’s no wonder the decentralized identity market is expected to jump from <a href="https://www.alliedmarketresearch.com/decentralized-identity-market-A27919">$156.8 million</a> in 2021 to a massive $77.8 billion by 2031 — that’s serious growth.</p><p>With decentralized identity, you only share what’s needed, keeping your information safer. It also saves time (faster logins or ID checks) and works everywhere, from banks to online services.</p><p>Take the European Union’s EUDI Wallet: it’s already helping people access their data across borders without the hassle.</p><h2>Future-proof your security strategy with Plivo</h2><p>Whether you're protecting internal systems or customer data, understanding MFA vs. 2FA is important for your business. 2FA requires users to prove their identity in two simple ways.
