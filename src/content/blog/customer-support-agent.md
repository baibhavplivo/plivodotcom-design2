This, in turn, strengthens customer trust and loyalty.</p><p>Support agents play a vital role in enhancing customer satisfaction—which indirectly drives ROI through repeat purchases—but they are not sales representatives or marketers. Marketers and sales reps acquire customers and sell.&nbsp;</p><p>On the other hand, support agents retain these customers and boost repeat purchases through efficient assistance.</p><h2>4 primary responsibilities of a support agent</h2><p>A support agent's primary responsibilities include serving as the first point of contact, handling queries, providing proactive support, and gathering essential feedback.</p><h3>1. Serving as a brand's first-line contact</h3><p>When things go wrong, customers become frustrated and demand to speak with anyone—even the CEO. Your support agents step in to act as the first line of defense, de-escalate situations, and provide solutions professionally.&nbsp;</p><p>They also represent your brand through every empathic response, ensuring customers leave with a positive experience and a lasting impression of excellent service.</p><h3>2. Handling customer inquiries</h3><p>Customer support agents handle all customer inquiries. Depending on the technicality of the query, they might sometimes involve a specialist or higher executive.&nbsp;</p><p>For instance, if a customer has a dispute over billing that involves policy exceptions, your agent might need to coordinate with or escalate to a higher executive for approval.</p><h3>3. This is why certain soft skills, beyond technical competency, are essential when recruiting one.</p><h3>Active listening and empathy</h3><p>Active listening involves paying close attention to every word and emotion communicated rather than focusing solely on the problem.&nbsp;</p><p>Unsurprisingly, active listeners excel at expressing empathy since they’re more likely to understand how customers feel better. This deeper connection allows them to communicate effectively, provide reassurance, and de-escalate tense situations.</p><h3>Good communication skills</h3><p>A good support agent must convey their points concisely while considering the appropriate tone.&nbsp;</p><p>They know when to avoid passive-aggressive words such as “actually, ought to,” colloquialisms, and slang to maintain clarity.</p><figure style="max-width:1034px" data-rt-max-width="1034px"><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/656ac26f3a3f6e1cc08ba7a5/67fe0a741f19b9ba3dcc39a4_67fcb8df2375ebb329c0bb30_AD_4nXeQLT5rAGEPN1qC9GOQAgvsd_BAd3SxdWqnHW3uwbUX7VisZkZKrNoouxG1bLlWMi5Mi2Ew3xrcts93IxNMuCAy6soG-qB6_vdNgi_9vi2kp5H_2ranySnlW2qdTL-vQYw.png" alt="Comparison between negative and positive communication tone" width="auto" height="auto" loading="auto"></div><figcaption>Good support agents adopt a positive communication tone to address customer complaints</figcaption></figure><h3>Adaptability</h3><p>Different customers present with different complaints, personalities, and communication styles. Some are patient and easy to communicate with, whereas some are aggressive and impatient.&nbsp;</p><p>So, a one-size-fits-all approach won’t work, and that’s why an ideal support agent must be able to adapt to each high and low while maintaining efficient support delivery.</p><h3>Problem-solving skills</h3><p>Customer support playbooks are valuable, but not every customer complaint will fit neatly into predefined solutions. Situations like this require your support agent to think outside the box while staying within your company’s guardrails.&nbsp;</p><p>A good support agent must be able to devise quick solutions to new problems and escalate as soon as possible when necessary.</p><h2>6 steps to hire the right support agent and build a functional support team</h2><p>Your support team can either make or mar your business. Prioritize agents with empathy and problem-solving skills</h3><p>You need a team of agents who can dialogue with different kinds of customers, resonate with them emotionally, and make them feel heard while providing adequate support. That’s why you should prioritize agents with empathy.</p><p>Also, look for agents with strong problem-solving skills—someone who can think quickly on their feet and provide solutions to out-of-the-script problems.</p><p>Tools like <a href="https://www.testgorilla.com/test-library/role-specific-skills-tests/customer-service-test/">TestGorilla</a> and <a href="https://testlify.com/test-library/customer-service-agent-test/">Testify</a> help you assess your applicant’s skills and ability to address different scenarios. For a more tailored assessment, present candidates with real scenarios or past customer complaints from your database and ask them to resolve the issue.</p><h3>3. Test for relevant technical competency</h3><p>Technical competency is your support agent’s ability to resolve customers’ technical queries. This could include troubleshooting software issues and providing answers to product-specific technical queries.</p><p>Usually, your agents don’t need to be tech geeks or specialists for L1 tickets. An entry-level customer support agent with the necessary soft skills can do a great job here.</p><p>However, it’s a different ball game for agents managing L2 and L3 tickets. They should have the necessary expertise to manage complex and product-related technical issues like specialists.&nbsp;</p><p>This primarily hinges on their hard skills portfolio, which you need to consider when hiring.
