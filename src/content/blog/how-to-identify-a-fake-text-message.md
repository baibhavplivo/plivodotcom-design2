<p>Text message scams, or <strong>smishing</strong>, are becoming increasingly sophisticated, making it harder to tell if a message is real or fake. More than <a href="https://keepnetlabs.com/blog/smishing-statistics-2023-the-latest-trends-and-numbers-in-sms-phishing">3.5 billion phone users</a> receive spam text messages that trick individuals into sharing sensitive information, such as credit card details, social security numbers, and banking credentials.&nbsp;</p><p>While many think they can spot a scam from a mile away. But, in reality, only less than <a href="https://www.safetydetectives.com/blog/what-is-smishing-sms-phishing-facts/">35% of the population</a> knows what smishing is. These fraudulent messages can look shockingly legitimate.&nbsp;</p><p>In this post, we’ll show you how to identify a fake text message, and provide tips on how to protect yourself.</p><h2><strong>What is smishing?</strong></h2><p><strong>Smishing</strong> is a form of phishing conducted via text messages. These fraudulent SMS attempts often appear to come from trusted entities—your bank, government agencies, even friends or family—convincing you to share private information or click on malicious links.</p><p>The goal of smishing is to steal your sensitive data or install malicious software on your device. These texts often play on emotions, like urgency or fear, to prompt quick action.</p><h3><strong>What are some common traits of scam text messages?</strong></h3><ol><li><strong>Unsolicited texts from unknown or spoofed numbers<br></strong>A typical characteristic of a scam text is that it comes from an unfamiliar number. While legitimate businesses use <a href="https://www.plivo.com/sms/toll-free/">shortcodes or toll-free numbers</a>, scammers often spoof these to make the text appear trustworthy. If you receive a text from a random, long-numbered, or suspicious source, take extra caution.</li><li><strong>Urgent requests or alarming content<br></strong>Fake texts often claim urgent issues like unauthorized bank transactions, problems with accounts, or family emergencies that require immediate action. Scammers know that stress and fear can cloud your judgment, so they pressure you into taking quick, unthought-out actions.</li><li><strong>Strange grammar or formatting<br></strong>Look for signs like spelling errors, awkward phrasing, or inconsistent formatting. Real businesses take care in crafting their messages, while scammers might rush or use poor language to disguise their intentions.</li></ol><h2><strong>What are the common types of text message scams?</strong></h2><p>Understanding the types of scams is key to recognizing them before it’s too late. Here are a few common examples:</p><ol><li><strong>Missed delivery notifications<br></strong>You might receive a text claiming that a package was undeliverable and prompting you to visit a suspicious website or call a fake customer service number.</li><li><strong>Prize or sweepstakes notifications<br></strong>A scammer might tell you that you’ve won a prize but need to pay a fee or provide personal details to claim it. Be especially wary if you’ve never entered any contests.</li><li><strong>Bank account closure threats<br></strong>Scammers pretending to be from your bank may warn that your account is about to be locked unless you take action immediately. These texts often include fake links or phone numbers to collect your login details.</li><li><strong>Subscription service payment failures<br></strong>If you receive a text claiming a failed payment for a service you use, be cautious. Scammers use this tactic to extract payment information or prompt you to visit malicious websites.</li></ol><h2><strong>How do scammers use text messages?</strong></h2><figure><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/656ac26f3a3f6e1cc08ba7a5/67ab16f8a04c8b20cfc29098_AD_4nXfCTyyU7KE6w0ludK-Fc0snaf2xat8oofXUAf1tCCWe32EW_WkfkLQmZmlVNdI0nfi1o4y_LgL0uWaduxaPWlVSGpx3NMh4kgrmh4RW8Mow3-TS8snAPblb_wjtn5vUS3FPYLjuew.png" width="auto" height="auto" alt="" loading="auto"></div></figure><p>Scammers typically rely on tactics that mimic trusted brands or entities to trick victims into responding. These tactics include:</p><ol><li><strong>Impersonating reputable companies or individuals</strong>: Scammers may pose as well-known organizations like your bank, delivery services, or government agencies to gain your trust.</li><li><strong>Spoofing phone numbers</strong>: By making it appear as though a trusted number is sending the message, they increase the likelihood of you believing the scam.</li><li><strong>Social engineering</strong>: Scammers tailor their messages to seem more believable using information gathered from previous data breaches or public sources.</li><li><strong>Unsolicited messages with suspicious links or requests<br></strong>Unsolicited messages containing unexpected links or requests should always be approached with caution. Pay attention to details like URLs that closely mimic legitimate domains but include slight variations, such as additional characters or unfamiliar extensions.&nbsp;</li></ol><p>Avoid clicking on links in these messages. Instead, verify the authenticity by directly visiting the organization's website through a trusted search engine or contacting them via official channels.&nbsp;</p><h2><strong>How to identify scam text messages?</strong></h2><figure><div><img src="https://cdn.prod.website-files.com/656ac26f3a3f6e1cc08ba7a5/67ab16f8bdd618553f795707_AD_4nXeFtqsN9V6werymCjTRFxzRHGfDo8UdgUYm6NekBuDFAEzzkQhMAJH324cOBrGhLxtpq7cN50g0pFXO_cszASER3MQ8PAfYb-XwnVmCn9aunPrbg3Rdvq7fKav_kIqVTr7L8kg-.png" width="auto" height="auto" alt="" loading="auto"></div></figure><h2><strong>What protective measures can you take against text scams?</strong></h2><p>To avoid falling victim to smishing attacks, follow these protective steps:</p><ol><li><strong>Avoid responding to suspicious texts<br></strong>Don’t engage with any message you suspect to be a scam.
