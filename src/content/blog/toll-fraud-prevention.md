<p>The <a href="https://cfca.org/wp-content/uploads/2023/11/Global-Fraud-Loss-Survey-2023.pdf">Global Fraud Loss Survey 2023</a> found that robocall scams in North America now account for more than 50% of global losses. Yet, many businesses are unprepared to deal with robocall scams and their implications for customers.&nbsp;</p><p>As businesses increasingly rely on automated systems and digital communication, the opportunities for fraudsters to exploit vulnerabilities also rise.&nbsp;</p><p>Toll fraud not only affects customer trust but can lead to significant financial losses and damage to a company's reputation. It is crucial for businesses to understand the mechanisms of such frauds and implement robust prevention strategies to safeguard their operations and maintain customer confidence.</p><p>In this guide, we’ll dive deeper into toll fraud and explain how to take steps to protect your business.</p><h2>What is toll fraud?</h2><p>Toll fraud, otherwise called international revenue sharing fraud (IRSF), occurs when an unauthorized individual gains control of a company’s phone system to transfer long-distance, international, or even premium-rate calls at the owner's expense.&nbsp;</p><p>Usually, fraudsters gain access to voice mail systems or private branch exchanges (PBXs) that are not adequately secured. They use this access to make call-through calls, which rack up expenses by using unreasonable foreign or premium services.&nbsp;</p><h2>How does toll fraud work?</h2><p>Toll fraud affects landline and mobile numbers in more than 200 countries. The profitable nature of toll fraud is reflected in the emergence and re-selling of the number range, as many as 10,000 new IRSF-related areas are promoted weekly.</p><p>Here’s a step-by-step example of how toll fraud works to the detriment of a business.&nbsp;</p><p><strong>Finding weaknesses:</strong> Fraudsters scour telecommunication systems for vulnerabilities. These weaknesses could involve poorly secured account registration processes or weak security settings.</p><ol><li><strong>Fake account factory:</strong> Once a weakness is identified, the fraudster creates many fake accounts using automated bots or scripts. These fake accounts can appear quite legitimate at first glance.</li><li><strong>Premium number power:</strong> Fraudsters leverage premium-rate or premium-service numbers provided by telecom carriers. These numbers charge significantly higher rates per call or text message compared to regular numbers.</li><li><strong>Exploiting the system:</strong> Fraudsters use the fake accounts and premium numbers to steal money using two different methods:<br><ul><li><strong>The 2FA trap:</strong> If a service uses SMS or voice verification codes for two-factor authentication (2FA), fraudsters can exploit this. They trigger a massive number of verification code requests to be sent to their fake accounts, all routed to the premium numbers. This results in a surge of expensive texts or calls being billed.</li><li><strong>Account activity abuse:</strong> Services with free trials or account creation are vulnerable. Fraudsters exploit weak registration processes to create a multitude of fake accounts. These accounts might then be used to trigger actions that generate SMS or voice traffic to premium numbers, racking up charges for the service provider.</li></ul></li><li><strong>Profit sharing scheme:</strong> In some cases, the fraudsters might collude with a complicit telecom carrier. The carrier might share a portion of the inflated revenue generated from the premium number usage.</li></ol><p>By generating a massive amount of fake traffic to premium numbers, fraudsters steal money, often leaving the service provider or unsuspecting user with a hefty bill.</p><h2>Who is at risk of toll fraud?</h2><p>Voice over internet protocol (VoIP) users, firms using premium rate numbers, and people dealing with international communications are most vulnerable to the threat of toll fraud.</p><h3>Industries and businesses at risk</h3><h4>1. VoIP Users</h4><p><strong>Volume</strong>: High</p><p>Fraudsters frequently target VoIP users. Because of its digital nature, VoIP software is more prone to manipulation than other telecommunication methods.</p><h4>2. Users of premium-rate numbers</h4><p><strong>Volume</strong>: Very high</p><p>Businesses using premium-rate phone numbers are vulnerable to toll fraud. Fake helpline numbers are designed to charge callers more than they would pay if they directly called any other number. Fraudsters redirect callers to these helplines and collect the revenue.</p><p>Examples: adult chat lines, tech support lines, interactive voting systems</p><h4>3. <strong>Companies with international call facilities</strong></h4><p><strong>Volume</strong>: Medium to high</p><p>Remote or international businesses, as well as those who are in the habit of making calls overseas, fall under the high-risk category as well. Fraudsters divert these calls to the high-cost destinations of their choice to pocket a maximum of the bogus billing payments.</p><p>For example, a U.S. tech company discovers that its phone system has been hacked, with all calls being redirected to premium-rate numbers in Latvia and the Maldives. Overnight, the sudden surge in international calls reveals significant financial misuse. This anomaly allowed the breach to be detected quickly.</p><h4>4. <strong>High-risk geographic locations</strong></h4><p><strong>Volume</strong>: High</p><p>Fraudsters direct calls mainly to countries that are known for their comparatively expensive rates.
