<p>A customer calls with a question. Before they even finish asking, one artificial intelligence (AI) agent is already listening, another is digging through past chats, and a third is crafting the perfect response.</p><p>It’s similar to having a team of experts working behind the scenes — fast, efficient, and always on point.</p><p>That’s the power of multi-agent artificial intelligence.</p><p>In 2025, AI isn’t a lone worker anymore. Companies like Google DeepMind are pushing it further with projects like <a href="https://deepmind.google/discover/blog/sima-generalist-ai-agent-for-3d-virtual-environments/">Scalable Instructable Multiworld Agent (SIMA)</a>, where AI agents team up to follow human instructions in 3D virtual worlds. They’re training these agents to explore, build, and solve problems in video games, adapting to new tasks as a group.</p><p>When these AI agents work together, they handle challenges faster and better than a single agent could. Curious how this is changing things? Keep reading to find out!</p><h2>How AI agents team up</h2><p>While a single AI agent can be helpful, the real power of AI emerges when multiple agents work together. These systems bring specialized agents with unique skills to tackle complex or large-scale tasks that would be difficult for one agent to handle alone. This teamwork makes it easier for organizations to automate and improve their processes.</p><p>Here’s how multi-agent AI systems work:</p><ul><li><strong>Understanding requests:</strong> One or more agents process the input, breaking it down to determine intent and key details.</li><li><strong>Planning workflows:</strong> Another set of agents maps out the necessary steps, assigning tasks to the right agents.</li><li><strong>Coordinating the team:</strong> A dedicated agent ensures that all AI agents communicate and work in sync.</li><li><strong>Executing tasks:</strong> Specialized agents handle their assigned steps, whether retrieving data, generating responses, or performing calculations.</li><li><strong>Collaborating with humans:</strong> If human input is needed, an agent flags the task and integrates their feedback.</li><li><strong>Validating outputs:</strong> Before delivering a final response, agents check for accuracy, consistency, and relevance.</li></ul><p>These systems often combine standard agents like those handling user requests or managing data with specialized agents that have unique tools or skills, such as pulling data or interpreting images. Together, they work toward a goal you set.</p><p>At the heart of every agent is a large language model (LLM). This helps them understand what you’re saying and the situation around it.</p><p>Depending on the task, all agents might use the same model, or each could use a different one. This setup lets some agents share what they know while others double-check the work, making everything more reliable and consistent.</p><p>The system gets even better with shared memory. It stores information for the short- and long-term. This cuts down on how often humans need to step in during planning, checking, or refining a project.</p><p>Here’s the process in action:</p><ul><li>The system takes a complicated task and breaks it into smaller, more manageable parts.</li><li>It assigns each task to the agent best equipped to handle it.</li><li>Agents and humans collaborate seamlessly throughout the process.</li></ul><h2>Where you’ll see AI agents in action</h2><p>AI agents use machine learning (ML) and advanced algorithms to make decisions, interact with diverse environments, and adapt to changing conditions. These systems are changing industries by making work faster, more accurate, and tailored to people’s needs.</p><p>Here’s how AI agents are helping out in different areas, with real examples of them in action.</p><h3>Customer service</h3><p>Businesses often deal with lots of customer questions and need to help people who speak different languages. When training massive models like GPT-3, they churn out greenhouse gases equivalent to what several cars would produce over many lifetimes.</p><p>Even a single chat with one of these models can use up to <a href="https://www.polytechnique-insights.com/en/columns/energy/generative-ai-energy-consumption-soars/">10 times more electricity</a> than a quick Google search.</p><p>Looking ahead, <a href="https://www.cell.com/joule/fulltext/S2542-4351(23)00365-3?_returnURL=https%3A%2F%2Flinkinghub.elsevier.com%2Fretrieve%2Fpii%2FS2542435123003653%3Fshowall%3Dtrue">experts predict</a> that AI could be using as much power as a small country like Ireland. That’s a lot to wrap your head around!</p><p>For businesses relying on AI agents, say, for writing customer replies or generating healthcare reports, this ramps up both their energy bills and their environmental footprint.</p><p>To tackle this, opt for smarter solutions like designing energy-efficient algorithms, using specialized AI chips, and switching data centers to renewable power sources.</p><h3>Privacy and ethics</h3><p>AI agents use huge amounts of data to do their jobs. But here’s where it gets tricky: when that data gets shared, privacy and ethical questions pop up fast.</p><p>Picture a customer service bot passing along your chat details or a healthcare agent dealing with your personal health stats. If that info isn’t handled carefully, it could end up in the wrong hands or be misused.</p><p>AI often makes decisions without explaining how it reached them. This lack of transparency can hide biases and lead to unfair outcomes.</p><p><a href="https://www.isaca.org/resources/news-and-trends/isaca-now-blog/2021/beware-the-privacy-violations-in-artificial-intelligence-applications">Research</a> from the Information Systems Audit and Control Association (ISACA), highlights how this lack of openness is a real problem.</p><p>So, who’s keeping an eye on these systems? That’s the big question.</p><p>The solution is strong oversight, clear regulations like the General Data Protection Regulation (EU), and greater transparency in AI. People have a right to know how their data is used, and AI systems should be able to explain their decisions.</p><p>That’s the key to keeping things fair and safe.</p><h3>Costs and complexity</h3><p>Building an AI agent takes careful planning, design, coding, testing, and finally, deployment. Each step requires skilled experts and a well-planned budget to bring it to life.</p><p>Scaling them comes with issues such as inconsistent data quality and rising costs, as <a href="https://www.mckinsey.com/industries/technology-media-and-telecommunications/our-insights/the-promise-and-the-reality-of-gen-ai-agents-in-the-enterprise#:~:text=So%20far%2C%20adoption%20at%20scale%20across%20businesses%20has%20faced%20difficulties%20because%20of%20data%20quality%2C%20employee%20distrust%2C%20and%20cost%20of%20implementation.%20In%20addition%2C%20capabilities%20have%20raced%20ahead%20of%20leaders%E2%80%99%20capacity%20to%20imagine%20how%20these%20agents%20could%20be%20used%20to%20transform%20work.">McKinsey</a> highlights.</p><p>The complexity comes from needing top-notch experts, massive computing power, and constant training. For example, Meta’s LLaMA 2 took millions of GPU hours to train, racking up millions in hardware costs alone.</p><p>But there’s hope: businesses can cut corners (in a good way!) by using pre-trained models, tapping into cloud services, or grabbing open-source tools. These tricks bring the price down and make the process less of a headache.</p><p>And as more companies bring in generative AI agents, costs will likely reduce. This could open the door to new customer experience (CX) options, like offering human support as a premium service for those who want a more personal touch.</p><h2>Trends to watch</h2><p>As we look ahead, AI agents are gearing up to play an even bigger role in our lives.
